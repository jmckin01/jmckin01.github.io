Research Summary

I am interested in studying the evolution of galaxies over the history of the Universe. Ten billion years ago, the density of star formation peaked and individual galaxies were forming more stars than at any other time in cosmic history. At this so-called "cosmic noon", galaxies host rich reservoirs of gas, and may also convert gas into stars more efficiently. A change in the efficiency of star-formation may or may not contribute to its overall peak at cosmic noon. Indeed, galaxies with unusually high star-formation rates for their stellar mass (starbursts) are more efficient at forming their stars, seemingly independent of redshift. Why are these galaxies more efficient at forming their stars? The physical basis for more, or less, efficient star-formation remains unclear, and the answer lies in the details of energy transfer in galaxies. Dissipative processes allow interstellar dust and gas to cool and form stars, which then re-inject heat through radiation and mechanical processes. The intimate link between star-formation and the interstellar medium manifests in global scaling relations like the Kennicut-Scmidt law, and the physical evolution of the interstellar medium must therefore drive changes in the star-formation rates of galaxies.

To better understand the link between the gas in galaxies and star-formation, I focus my research on characterizing the physical conditions of star-forming gas in the most actively star-forming galaxies in the local Universe and at cosmic noon. I use data from a wide range of telescope facilities including ALMA, HST, Spitzer, the VLA, and Keck, and I have extensive experience reducing raw interferometric data.
